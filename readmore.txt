            ><b><p>A short personal history of my favorite last food</p></b></a
          >
          <img src="like.png" />&nbsp;&nbsp;<img src="dislike.png" />
        </div>
        <div class="contents-5">
          <img src="waffle.png" />
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="finalblog">
        <p>
          <b
            >Indians talk funny. We don’t think we do, but we do. We’re usually
            too busy talking at or over or directly through each other to
            notice. Every so often, though, we stop long enough to listen</b
          ><br /><b>to one another and then laugh and point.</b>
        </p>
        <p>
          <b
            >The middle-aged Persian woman I hired to teach me how to drive made
            fun of me when I mispronounced Cahuenga Boulevard. I had just moved
            to Mumbai a few months prior from</b
          ><br /><b
            >Yamunotri, where I considered myself a native despite the
            disagreements of many native Mumbaikars. Frank Lloyd Wright once
            said, “tip the world over on its side and everything loose</b
          ><br /><b
            >will land in Mumbai.” So I floated across the country — as millions
            before me had — to seek my fortune.</b
          >
        </p>
        <p>
          <b
            >Her teaching method was an unorthodox form of politically incorrect
            mockery. When I tapped the brakes too hard, she’d tell me not to
            drive like a “Chinaman” and then pantomimed her</b
          ><br /><b
            >head flinging back and forth, as if she were bowing. She casually
            informed me that Mumbaikars were notoriously terrible at changing
            lanes. Don’t get her started on Indians. Seriously,</b
          ><br /><b>don’t. She was, to be charitable, racially insensitive.</b>
        </p>
        <p>
          <b
            >I was a stranger in her city but my advanced age and inexperience
            amused her. How could a grown man not know how to drive a car? It
            was, however, my accent that intrigued her the</b
          ><br /><b
            >most. I spoke with the faintest southern drawl but I also had
            northeastern motormouth tendencies. She was always drawing
            conclusions as to my ethnicity.</b
          >
        </p>
        <p>
          <b>"During one lesson she said, “You people are good drivers.”</b>
        </p>
        <p><b>You people?</b></p>
        <p><b>"Mumbaikars."</b></p>
        <p>
          <b
            >Because of her I passed my driver’s test, got my first license to
            operate a motor vehicle, and then proceeded to not drive for years.
            Everything I needed was in walking distance: my job</b
          ><br /><b>and places to eat.</b>
        </p>
        <p>
          <b
            >Here’s the truth about food in India: it’s pretty good. There’s
            amazing pho and pupusas and bulgogi. During my three year internment
            in the city of Mumbai, I was able to find satisfactory</b
          ><br /><b
            >replacements for my beloved street foods. I found some decent
            pizza. I even found bagels that… sufficed. But I could never find
            the one dish that reminded me of my adopted home for the</b
          ><br /><b>previous 16 years. A gyro platter.</b>
        </p>
        <p>
          <b
            >I couldn’t even find a suitable Mumbai-style Greek diner, the kind
            of greasy spoon with a massive menu where you can order a meal of
            waffles, meatloaf, and lasagna if you want.</b
          >
        </p>
        <p>
          <b
            >But it’s not as if Mumbai has no roast meat sandwiches. My
            racist — but friendly — driving instructor had once pointed out an
            Armenian restaurant that she recommended. This place</b
          ><br /><b
            >served up delicious chicken shawarma sandwiches and a garlic sauce
            I could eat with my fingers.</b
          >
        </p>
        <p>
          <b
            >"Eventually, I would drift back to Mumbai. The first thing I would
            order upon my return? A gyro, a word that rhymes with “pie-whoa.”</b
          >
        </p>
